About This Workbook

This pamphlet offers a set of exercises, including prompts for a daily research and reflection journal, to guide you in the Field Workshop program.

The prompts are fully optional, but we hope they will sharpen your inquiry and give us, as a group, a shared foundation to draw from as we discuss our process throughout the workshop period.

We have also included a number of prompts that we hope you will respond to and hand in to us at the end of the Field Workshop, including a reflection on your synthesis group projects.

Over three weeks, your reflections will become a record of your development as an integrative researcher and will also provide the Institute with crucial feedback that will enable us to continue to refine and iterate the Field Workshop program in the future.

Exercise 1: Coming from Elsewhere

We begin at the beginning, by placing ourselves in relation to the Field Workshop site. Most people who join this workshop are not from the San Luis Valley or the Taos Plateau — even if we are returning to the Valley, we bring new perspectives and learning from elsewhere. We are all arriving with credentials, curiosity, and good intentions, and an outside perspective, which are gifts to bring into our interactions and experiences within the region.

While it is possible that some of the communities we will meet have experience with researchers who arrive, extract, and leave, they may also have experienced researchers who arrived in the valley and became genuine long-term partners. While your time in this region may not extend past the Field Workshop, Wright-Ingraham will continue working in the region for the coming years, and you can anticipate that your research here will spark future collaborations and conversations.

We offer the following prompt to generate thoughts and interest in how your own positionality might fit into this place for the time you are here.

What does it mean to you to come from outside (or are returning to) the communities you are about to enter as a researcher?

What assumptions are you carrying about these places and the people who live and work in them — and where did those assumptions come from?

What do you have to offer these communities, honestly, as someone who is new (or returning) here? What are the limits of what you can offer in three weeks?

What would you want a researcher to do — and not do — if they were coming to your home community to study something you care about? What would help you to trust them?

Exercise 3: Optional Daily Reflection Prompts

We suggest you use the daily reflection prompts as a practice for you to record your observations and experiences during the Field Workshop. These prompts recall our Integration Cycle detailed in the Integrate This! handbook.

Date: ___________________________    Location: _________________________________________

Immerse
What did you observe, encounter, or experience today? What is your first, unfiltered impression of where you were and what you witnessed? What did your body notice before your mind caught up — the light, the scale, the texture of the place, the quality of a conversation?
Diverge
What different lenses or framings could you apply to what you saw? Whose perspective were you missing today? What disciplinary or community lens, if you had access to it, would make what you observed look completely different?
Connect
What surprised you? Where did different threads link? Where did something you heard or saw today connect unexpectedly with something from a different part of your experience — another field site, another conversation, something you read, something you already knew? What is the connection telling you?
Synthesize
What is your provisional understanding today? What question emerged? Complete this sentence without overthinking it: “Today I learned that…” Then: “The question I’m carrying into tomorrow is…”

Exercise 4: Transect Exercise

A transect is a line that cuts through a landscape. Functioning as a cross-section, it allows us to observe how environmental conditions, land uses, species, infrastructures, and human activities vary across space. By moving between close observation and broader patterns, transects can reveal relationships that are not immediately visible from a single viewpoint.

Throughout the workshop, the transect can be used as a tool for investigating a place, a question, or a relationship that interests you. Think of it as one of several methods available to support your learning, observation, and project development.

Prompt 1: Draw the Transect

Begin with a point of interest. Spend time observing the site closely. From this point, construct a transect line that extends through the landscape — it may intersect with shifts in vegetation, elevation, water flow, movement of people, governance systems, habitat corridors, infrastructure, or other relationships that interest you.

Consider:

  • What lies upstream and downstream?
  • What lies above and below?
  • What is visible and invisible?
  • What flows through this place?
  • What people, species, materials, or systems are connected to it?
  • What forces have shaped the conditions you observe?

Record your observations through sketches, drawing, and annotation. If time allows, incorporate photography, collage, collected materials, or other visual methods. The transect does not need to be geographically exact. It may combine direct observation, conversation, research, memory, and speculation to reveal connections that are otherwise difficult to see.

Exercise 5: Shared Mapping Exercise

Throughout the workshop, we will gather observations, stories, questions, drawings, diagrams, photographs, and insights from site visits, conversations, fieldwork, transects, and project development. At the end of each workshop phase, we will come together to collectively map what we have learned.

Using a shared base map, tracing paper, annotation, drawing, and collage, participants will contribute observations and connections from their individual experiences. As layers are added, patterns, relationships, and questions may begin to emerge that were not visible from any single perspective.

Prompt: Contribute to the Map

Bring what you have gathered — field observations, transect drawings, diagram fragments, questions, contradictions — and add it to the shared map. Look at what others have contributed. Follow a connection that surprises you.

Consider:

  • What themes appear across multiple sites?
  • What relationships connect people, places, and systems?
  • What tensions, contradictions, or dependencies become visible?
  • What questions remain unresolved?
  • What knowledge is missing from the map?

The purpose of the map is not to produce a definitive representation of the San Luis Valley. It is a tool for exploration, collective learning, and shared inquiry. Use it to identify connections, surface different perspectives, and deepen your understanding of the landscapes and communities encountered throughout the workshop.

What did you contribute to the map? What did you notice in others' contributions?

Exercise 6: Systems Diagram Exercise

Prompt 1: Draw a Systems Diagram

Using what you have observed, read, and heard during the workshop, draw a causal loop diagram of drought in the San Luis Valley.

Begin by identifying three to five variables that seem important to you — these might include aquifer level, snowpack, groundwater pumping, soil cover, crop decisions, institutional response, community trust, or anything else that has surfaced in your work. Draw arrows between them. Label each arrow with a '+' or '−'. Follow the arrows back until you find loops that close.

Consider:

  • What reinforces the problem — where does a change in one variable make things worse?
  • What resists it — where are the balancing mechanisms, and why might they be insufficient?
  • Where are the delays? Where does cause and effect come apart in time?
  • What is the stock you are most concerned about — aquifer level, topsoil, institutional trust — and what are the flows that deplete or replenish it?
  • What is driving the system's behavior that no single actor intended?

Your diagram does not need to be complete or correct. It is a thinking tool, not a truth claim. Draw with the understanding that it will be revised. Mark the parts you are uncertain about. Note the variables you suspect are important but do not yet understand well enough to connect.

If time allows, share your diagram with a partner. Ask: does this match what you have seen? What would you add, move, or remove? What does your partner's diagram reveal that yours does not?

Prompt 2: Identify a Leverage Point

Return to your diagram and ask: where might a small change produce a significant shift in the system's behavior?

Donella Meadows observed that the most obvious interventions — adjusting numbers, changing the size of a fee or a quota — are typically the least effective. The more powerful leverage points are structural: the rules that govern feedback loops, the information flows that inform decisions, the goals that institutions are organized around, and, most powerful of all, the paradigm from which the system arises.

Mark the leverage point you find most interesting. Write a sentence or two about why you chose it, and what kind of intervention it might suggest. Bring this with you to the group mapping session.
